Flip a coin. Those are roughly the odds you can tell, by ear, whether a voice on a call is a real human or an AI. Research on audio deepfake detection puts human accuracy near 54% — barely better than chance.

That's unsettling, because we've built decades of trust — banking, account recovery, support, executive sign-off — on the assumption that a recognizable voice means a verified person. Global losses tied to voice-based fraud have already been estimated to exceed $1.8 billion.

Two old locks are quietly open. SMS one-time passwords can be lifted through SIM swaps and weaknesses in aging telecom routing. And static voiceprints? Modern models can clone a convincing voice from seconds of audio, turning "my voice is my password" into the combination to a safe the attacker has already memorized.

The credible response isn't a better single check. It's continuous, multimodal verification — analyzing the sound itself, cross-checking audio against lip movement, and never stopping at "hello."

If your organization still leans on OTPs or one-shot voice checks for high-risk moments, it's worth mapping what continuous, privacy-preserving verification would look like for you.
