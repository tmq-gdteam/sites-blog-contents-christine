Most contact centers track handle time, CSAT, and turnover. Few track the thing quietly inflating all three: acoustic friction.

Every "sorry, can you repeat that?" looks trivial. But background noise, unfamiliar accents, and laggy audio force the brain into constant prediction — and that work is exhausting. Spend eight to ten hours a day fighting unclear audio and the depletion is real. It's one reason the industry still wrestles with high agent turnover.

For years the "fix" was manual accent neutralization — asking offshore agents to suppress their inflection, adopt anglicized names, and memorize Western phrasing. This is where the topic gets genuinely contested. Critics, particularly applied linguists, call it a form of erasure. Others argue real-time accent harmonization can shield agents from bias while sparing them that exhausting self-suppression. Both sides deserve an honest hearing.

That tension is exactly why how you adopt voice AI matters as much as whether you do. The goal worth holding onto: enhance communication, don't erase difference.
