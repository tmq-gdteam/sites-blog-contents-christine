NotebookLM Podcast Transcript — "Texting Customers Without Annoying Them"

HOST A: Okay so, welcome back everyone. Today we're, uh, we're getting into something that I think a lot of small business owners kind of get wrong, which is, like, business texting. SMS. The whole thing.

HOST B: Yeah, totally. And I think the, the big misconception is that texting customers is somehow spammy or invasive. But the data — and just, you know, common sense — it points the other way. People actually prefer it. Like, way more than email.

HOST A: Right, because nobody opens email anymore.

HOST B: I mean, open rates for email, you're looking at, what, 20% if you're lucky? Text messages, almost everybody opens them. Within minutes usually. So if you actually need a response — confirming an appointment, you know, "is this time still good for you" — text just works.

HOST A: So why do so many businesses still not do it?

HOST B: A few reasons. One is they're scared of being annoying. Which, fair. We've all gotten that, like, relentless promo text from some store you bought socks from once. So there's a, a fear there. The second thing is logistics — a lot of small teams are just texting from someone's personal cell phone, which doesn't scale and is a nightmare when that person goes on vacation.

HOST A: Oh yeah. The "Karen has the customer phone and Karen's at the beach" problem.

HOST B: Exactly. So the trick — and this is the part people miss — the trick is that good business texting is about being useful, not promotional. The texts people actually like are the functional ones. Appointment reminders. Order's ready. Quick questions. Your invoice is due. That stuff. People love that stuff because it saves them a phone call.

HOST A: So it's, like, the difference between a text that helps you versus a text that's selling to you.

HOST B: That's the whole thing, honestly. If every text you send earns its place — meaning the customer is glad they got it — you basically never have an annoyance problem. The businesses that get in trouble are the ones blasting "FLASH SALE" three times a week.

HOST A: What about the personal phone thing? Like practically, what do people do instead?

HOST B: Yeah so you want a shared business number that the whole team can see and respond from. So it's not tied to one person. Anyone on the team can pick up the conversation. And ideally you want to be able to see the history, so when a customer texts back you're not starting from zero.

HOST A: Makes sense. Any quick wins for someone just starting?

HOST B: Sure. Start with the boring functional stuff — confirmations and reminders. Don't lead with marketing. Always let people reply, like, it should be a real conversation not a broadcast. And, um, make it easy to opt out, that's both a legal thing and just basic respect. Do those things and you're already ahead of most businesses.

HOST A: Love it. Okay, that's a great place to wrap. Thanks everyone.
