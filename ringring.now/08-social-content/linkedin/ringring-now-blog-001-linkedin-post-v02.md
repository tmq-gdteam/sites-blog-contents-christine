The biggest obstacle to real-time AI translation in business isn't latency or language coverage. It's fear — and it's well-founded.

Put yourself in a high-stakes negotiation. Two specific anxieties show up:

First, the uncanny valley. If the cloned voice sounds even slightly off, you lose executive presence instantly. Nobody wants to sound like a 1990s GPS while closing a deal. The whole appeal of voice preservation is really charisma preservation.

Second — the dangerous one — hallucination. If the AI flawlessly replicates your warm, trustworthy voice but renders "net 30 payment terms" as "we'll pay you in 30 years," sounding like yourself won't save you from the fallout.

That's why selling raw capability isn't enough in this category. You have to earn trust, and you have to be careful with words: "human-like" and "context-aware," never "perfect" or "error-free." And you don't bury a privacy policy in the footer — you put a clear "your call data is never used to train our models" guarantee where buyers can see it.

The benchmark to beat isn't other apps. It's the human interpreter — expensive and hard to schedule, but trusted. To replace that, AI has to be faster, cheaper, and trustworthy enough that the absence of a human feels like an upgrade.
