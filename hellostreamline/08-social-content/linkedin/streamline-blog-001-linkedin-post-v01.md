Here's a claim that sounds backward but holds up: adding the newest, most expensive software to your company can make your employees slower. Not because the tools are bad — because they're fighting each other.

The average enterprise now runs around 101 distinct SaaS applications. It stops sounding absurd the moment you map it by department: HR has a recruiting portal and a separate payroll system; marketing runs separate platforms for email, social, and SMS; sales has a CRM, a dialer, and an enrichment tool. Companies bought point solutions — one tool per problem — and kept stacking.

The human cost shows up as context switching. Employees toggle between apps something like 1,200 times a day, and each switch is a mental gear change. Research estimates this "tool fatigue" drains roughly 44 hours per worker a year — more than a full work week lost just finding the right window.

Globally, fragmented software is blamed for something on the order of $1.2 trillion in lost productivity a year. The cause isn't a shortage of technology; it's that we built a decade of powerful tools without weaving them together.

The mandate is shifting from acquiring new capabilities to orchestrating the ones you already have.

If your team loses entire work weeks to tab-switching, the smartest next move isn't another subscription.
