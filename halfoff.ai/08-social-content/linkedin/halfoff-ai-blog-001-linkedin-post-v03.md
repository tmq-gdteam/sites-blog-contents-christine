SaaS repatriation sounds like an obvious win, which is exactly why the catches deserve honest attention. There are three.

"Free" open source isn't free — it's free like a free puppy. Hosting, DevOps, security patching, and uptime are expensive; rebuild a CRM in-house and five-year operating costs can eclipse the original licensing fees. The only version that pencils out is managed open source, where a provider absorbs the infrastructure and 2 a.m. patching for one predictable fee. That predictability — not "free" software — is what caps a CFO's exposure.

The legal frontier is real and partly untested. The lawful path is clean-room engineering, rooted in Computer Associates v. Altai (1992): copyright protects the specific expression of code, not the underlying idea. Human clean-room work is well-established; doing it with automated AI agents hasn't been tested in appellate courts — so demand ironclad contractual indemnification.

Migration is where most attempts die. A large majority of data-migration projects fail or blow their budgets. The technique that de-risks it is a parallel cutover using change-data-capture pipelines — validating the new system against real traffic for weeks before you hand over the keys.

Treat any provider's headline savings as claims to verify, not facts.
