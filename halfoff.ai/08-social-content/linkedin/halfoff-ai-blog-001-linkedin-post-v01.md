A trap rarely looks like a trap from the outside. A wire lobster trap has bait, an easy entrance, and gives you exactly what you wanted — until you try to turn around.

That's a fair description of what enterprise SaaS has become. Here's the math behind the squeeze: corporate IT budgets grow on the order of a few percent a year, while SaaS subscription prices have been inflating well into the double digits. When your budget grows ~3% and your largest operating expense grows ~11%, that's not a line-item problem — it's a slow-motion solvency problem. Companies end up cutting projects, or headcount, just to keep the software running.

What makes it sting more: much of that spend is waste. The average enterprise juggles hundreds of SaaS apps, and a large share of provisioned seats — often cited near half — go unused.

The structural alternative gaining traction is "SaaS repatriation": moving core workflows off closed, proprietary SaaS onto managed open-source platforms you actually own. It turns software from perpetually inflating rent back into a capitalized asset.

Even if you never migrate a system, understanding how this works changes the power dynamic in your next renewal. You stop being a captive audience.
