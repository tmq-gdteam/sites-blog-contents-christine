You're on the phone trying to cancel a subscription. You pause for half a second to breathe — and the automated voice talks right over you. You stop. It stops. Silence. Then you both start again at the exact same moment.

We all know that voice. It feels robotic and just plain broken.

Now look at your laptop, where a text AI writes working code in seconds. So why is AI brilliant in a chat box and clumsy the moment it's on a call?

It's not a shortage of intelligence. It's a misunderstanding of conversation itself.

Two mechanics separate "natural" from "broken," and neither is about a faster model:

Knowing when you're done talking. Systems that rely on a silence timer can't win — tighten it and the AI cuts you off mid-thought; loosen it and you bolt on a second of lag. The fix is reading grammar and intent in real time, so "book a flight to… um…" waits, but "book a flight to London" gets an instant reply.

Handling interruptions. When you cut in, the AI has to stop talking AND rewind its own memory to the exact word it was speaking — otherwise it assumes you heard a sentence you never did, and the conversation derails.

The messiness of human speech — the stutters, the backtracking, the interruptions — isn't the bug. It's the benchmark.
