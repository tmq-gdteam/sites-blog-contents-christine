Engineers don't distrust your AI because they don't understand it. They distrust it because they understand it too well.

They live in a deterministic world. Apply a known force to a known material and it bends the same way every time. Their work is math.

A large language model is the opposite — it's probabilistic, guessing the next plausible token. To someone designing an aerospace part or a submarine, "statistically likely" doesn't sound like intelligence. It sounds like dangerous randomness. And in their world, randomness is catastrophic.

So when a technical expert resists handing their workflow to "a chatbot," that hesitation isn't ignorance. It's good judgment.

The teams winning this audience aren't marketing past that fear. They're engineering around it: using AI only to interpret intent, then executing deterministically. Keeping a human in the loop. Making every action reversible, so a mistake costs nothing.

Trust, for this crowd, isn't a tagline you write. It's an architecture you build — and then show.

If you're selling powerful technology to people who've seen every overpromise before, the bar is higher than "it works." You have to prove it won't break their world before they'll let you near it.

How are you building proof — not just claims — into what you sell?
