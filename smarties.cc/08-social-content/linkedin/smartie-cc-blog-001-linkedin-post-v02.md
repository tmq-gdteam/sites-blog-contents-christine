A company spends a fortune on supercomputing to run advanced physics simulations — then watches its best PhD engineers spend most of their day clicking through drop-down menus.

It's like hiring a Michelin-star chef and forcing them to chop onions all day with a broken knife.

That's the quiet reality of a lot of computer-aided engineering. The specialized genius is there. It's just being spent operating software instead of doing the actual work.

This is exactly the gap "intent-driven" AI tools are trying to close: describe what you want in plain language, let the machine handle the clicks. The opportunity is huge.

But here's the tension. The people who'd benefit most are also the hardest to convince. Deep-tech engineers are deeply analytical and deeply skeptical — and they've watched fragile automation scripts break silently after a single interface update too many times to take "trust us" at face value.

So the real challenge isn't building the automation. It's earning permission to use it.

That means proving — visibly — that the tool won't corrupt their model, won't leak their proprietary data, and won't quietly waste expensive compute in the middle of the night.

If your most valuable users are stuck doing low-value work, the bottleneck is rarely the technology. It's trust.

What's the "broken knife" task your customers are quietly tolerating right now?
