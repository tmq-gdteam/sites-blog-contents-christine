If you're selling advanced AI to skeptical technical buyers, "it's 2,400x faster" is not your strongest line. It's the moment they close the tab.

Analytical audiences discount claims and reward evidence. Here's what actually moves them — drawn from how deep-tech teams are positioning intent-driven AI to engineers:

1. Separate the guessing from the doing. Use AI to interpret intent, then execute deterministically. The model translates; it doesn't drive.

2. Assistant, not autopilot. "Autopilot" implies the human goes to sleep — which triggers every fear they have. Keep them in the loop by design.

3. Make it reversible. If any action can be staged, reviewed, and instantly undone, the fear of irreversible damage disappears.

4. Show, don't tell. Don't describe the tool working — let them watch it, and let them inspect exactly what it did. Transparency you can replay beats transparency you promise.

5. Handle big numbers with caution. If you cite pilot results, label them as early pilot data and name the narrow case they apply to. Overstate once and you're branded a fraud.

6. Sell to the whole committee. The power user, the team lead, and the security gatekeeper each need a different reason to say yes. Miss one and the deal stalls.

In deep tech, credibility is the only currency — and you spend it the instant you overpromise.
