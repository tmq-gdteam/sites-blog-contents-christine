Talking to your computer is faster than typing. That part isn't hype.

We speak at around 150 words per minute and type at roughly 40 — and research suggests voice typing can cut hand and wrist muscle activity by up to 80%. For drafting and for sparing your wrists, voice is a real win.

But "frictionless" and "safe" aren't the same thing, and the gap is where the interesting questions live.

A few things worth knowing before you go voice-first:

→ The correction tax is real, but smaller than it used to be. Modern dictation models read context well enough that effective finalized speed often lands around 85–115 WPM — still well past typing.

→ Listening isn't always easier than reading. For dense, complex material, audio can actually raise cognitive load. You're held to the speaker's pace, with no quick glance back. Save read-aloud for casual content; read the hard stuff in silence.

→ Read-aloud is convenience, not accessibility. A true screen reader navigates a page's structure. A text-to-speech layer just voices the words. Don't confuse the two.

→ Your voice is biometric data. Voiceprints are treated like fingerprints under laws such as BIPA and GDPR. Before a tool listens everywhere, ask how it handles consent, encryption, and deletion.

Voice belongs in your workflow — matched to the tasks where it genuinely wins, with the harder questions asked up front.
