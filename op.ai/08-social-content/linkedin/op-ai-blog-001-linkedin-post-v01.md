Legacy automation has a blindness problem.

Traditional RPA bots navigate software by clicking fixed screen coordinates — specific pixels. It works until someone moves the equivalent of a trash can into the path: a minor UI update, a button shifted two pixels. The bot doesn't step around it. It falls over, and a multi-million-dollar process fails.

The first attempted fix — process mining — watches how work flows and produces a beautiful map of your inefficiencies. But it's a consultant who hands you a gorgeous poster of your mess without cleaning the room. It maps the work; it doesn't do it.

The more interesting shift (Agentic Process Automation) bridges the two: understand the work *and* execute it. The key technical move is reading meaning instead of pixels — the structural accessibility layer underneath the screen — so the agent understands the *intent* of an action and doesn't break when the interface changes.

But capability isn't the hard part. The hard part is the black box: no operations leader will let an autonomous agent touch a live ERP if they can't audit *why* it did what it did. The answer isn't "trust the AI" — it's verifiability: dry-run testing before touching real data, private deployment, and a signed audit trail for every action.

This is the gap op.ai is built to close.
