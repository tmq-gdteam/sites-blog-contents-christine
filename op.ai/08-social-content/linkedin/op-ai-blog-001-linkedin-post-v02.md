Selling enterprise AI automation is like selling an invisible worker.

You walk into a boardroom and ask risk-averse executives to let an autonomous agent touch their most sensitive financial systems. If it makes a mistake, money moves to the wrong place. So the real obstacle was never capability. It's trust.

The thing that kills these deals is the black box. With standard generative AI, a prompt goes in, opaque math happens, and an answer pops out — untraceable. No CTO signs off on that for a live ERP.

The way through isn't louder claims of "magic." Buyers are exhausted by hype. It's the opposite: verifiability. Run the process in the dark first — thousands of dry runs validating assumptions before a single real dollar moves. Deploy privately inside the client's own infrastructure. Don't train public models on their data. Log every action with a tamper-evident trail.

And here's the tell of an honest vendor: the best ones refuse to claim 100% autonomy. They name the small edge-case variance where a human should stay in the loop. Claiming perfection destroys credibility with sophisticated buyers; "highly autonomous, with a human for the rest" earns it.

Treat the impressive metrics as figures to verify, not guarantees — and ask how each was measured in an environment like yours.
