Let's talk about the word "free" in small-business software — because it's quietly become a trap.

You'll recognize the patterns even if you can't name the tools. A "free plan" with so few messages you burn through them just testing it. Credit limits that switch the bot off mid-conversation until you enter a card. Quality that silently degrades once you cross an invisible line — the capable model swapped for a dumb keyword-matcher, so one minute it's answering fluently and the next it's stuck on "I do not understand."

The common thread: many of these are enterprise tools wearing a small-business mask, using limits as a ticking clock to force an upgrade.

That's a problem, because the businesses that most need an always-on website assistant — local services, consultants, solo operators — are exactly the ones with no time to play IT admin or decode token quotas. They care about two things: will it capture the lead, and will it sound like my business?

The antidote is radical transparency: a free plan that's actually a real plan, no card and no trial countdown, your leads owned by you and never sold or used to train someone else's model.

Before you install any tool, read the fine print like it's trying to trick you. Often, it is.
