Here's the part of conversational AI nobody puts on the sales page: the moment you let a chatbot talk to customers, courts may treat it as a legally binding representative of your business.

In a 2024 Canadian tribunal case, Air Canada's chatbot invented a refund policy that didn't exist. The airline argued the bot was a separate entity responsible for its own statements. The tribunal rejected that outright and held the company responsible — and made it honor the fabricated policy. A 2026 German court went further, treating a chatbot's invented claims as legally indistinguishable from a CEO making them on TV.

Apply that to a small shop: if a free, ungoverned bot hallucinates a 90%-off code to please a visitor, you may be on the hook to honor it.

The good news is that a safe setup is well understood. It rests on two things: a privacy layer (explicit consent before collecting data, and a way to delete transcripts) and a governed AI engine using retrieval-augmented generation plus strict guardrails — so the bot answers only from your approved content and says "let me get a team member" when it doesn't know.

A "paste your URL, done in 30 seconds" pitch and "we configured consent and guardrails" can't both be fully true.
