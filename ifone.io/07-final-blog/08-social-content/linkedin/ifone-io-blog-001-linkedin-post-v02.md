You can build a genuinely powerful product and still lose the customer in the first three seconds. I think of it as a Ferrari parked in an abandoned garage.

Picture a tool that unifies every message you've ever sent and remembers the context of every relationship — serious engineering. Now picture its website: a dated template, a copyright year that hasn't moved in ages, and a browser security warning on load.

For a high-leverage buyer — an executive, a founder — that's not a minor blemish. It's a fatal one. When you ask someone to connect their confidential client emails and private messages, they judge the unseen software by the polish of what they can see. Broken infrastructure hygiene on the homepage implies a risky codebase behind it. A stale site signals an abandoned product.

The uncomfortable lesson: basic upkeep isn't cosmetic. It's the very first trust signal, and you don't get the chance to explain the engineering if the garage door is hanging off its hinges.

This matters most precisely when the product is most ambitious — like a personal CRM that also unifies your communications. The more you ask of a user's trust, the less you can afford a single crack in the presentation.

ifone.io sits exactly here: a powerful idea whose job is to *earn* that trust on sight.
